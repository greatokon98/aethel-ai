Ideas move fast. Fingers move slow.

There's a gap between what you think and what you can get down on paper. For most people, that gap costs them good ideas every single day.

Here's a better way: skip the typing entirely.

## Why speaking beats typing

The average person types about 40 words per minute. But they speak at about 150 words per minute.

That's nearly four times as fast.

When you speak your ideas instead of typing them, you capture more. You capture the nuance. The tone. The details you'd forget by the time you finished the first sentence.

## What you need

The tools for this have gotten surprisingly good. You don't need expensive equipment or complicated software. Just:

- A phone or computer with a microphone
- A notes app that supports dictation
- Or a dedicated voice-to-text tool

Most phones already have this built in. On an iPhone, open the keyboard and tap the microphone icon. On a computer, most writing tools have a "Dictation" option under the Tools menu.

## How to make it a habit

**Brain dump:** When you wake up, record a voice note about anything on your mind. Don't edit. Don't filter. Just talk.

**First drafts:** When you need to write something - an article, an email, a proposal - say it out loud first. Record yourself explaining what you want to say. Then clean it up.

**Walking meetings:** Have a thought during a walk? Pull out your phone and record a quick note. Your future self will thank you.

## The editing step

The key insight: dictation gives you a rough draft, not a finished product. The real magic happens when you take that rough draft and polish it.

Read through what you captured. Fix the grammar. Tighten the sentences. Add the structure.

What used to take you an hour now takes fifteen minutes of talking and fifteen minutes of editing.

## A quick tip

Speak in complete sentences. It sounds unnatural at first, but it saves editing time later. Pause between thoughts. Say "period" or "new paragraph" if you need to.

And don't worry about sounding perfect. The edit step is where it gets good. The speaking step is where it gets fast.
