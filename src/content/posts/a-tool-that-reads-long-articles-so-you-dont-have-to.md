You bookmarked it three weeks ago. "I'll read this later," you told yourself.

Later never came.

The internet is full of long, interesting articles. But who has time to read them all? The good news: you don't have to.

## The one-minute summary

Most long-form content follows the same structure:

- A problem or question
- Some background or context
- The main argument or finding
- Supporting evidence
- A conclusion

What if you could get just those parts, in a few sentences, without reading the whole thing?

That's exactly what summarization tools do. You give them a link or paste in text, and they return a short summary.

## How to use it

**Step 1:** Find a long article you want to read but haven't gotten to.
**Step 2:** Copy the URL or paste the text into a summarization tool.
**Step 3:** Read the 3-5 sentence summary.
**Step 4:** Decide if the full article is worth your time.

Most of the time, the summary is enough. Sometimes, it makes you want to read the whole thing. Either way, you've saved 10-15 minutes.

## Where to use this

- **Work reports:** Get the key findings from a 20-page document in 30 seconds
- **News:** Stay informed without reading every article
- **Research:** Quickly evaluate whether a source is useful before diving in
- **Email newsletters:** That weekly newsletter you never read? Now you can

## A real example

Last week I had six industry reports to review for a project. Each one was 15-30 pages. I ran each through a summarizer, got the key takeaways, and decided that two were worth reading fully.

What would have taken four hours took about twenty minutes. And I didn't miss anything important.

## The catch

Summaries are not replacements. They're filters.

If you're reading something that matters deeply - a contract, a technical specification, a loved one's writing - read the full thing. But for everything else? Let the tool do the heavy lifting.
